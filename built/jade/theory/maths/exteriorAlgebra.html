1 Exterior algebra

1.1 Exterior algebra

1.1.1 The exterior (wedge) product

The exterior product of two vectors is:

u ∧ v

1.1.2 The exterior product is anticommutative

This is anticommutative (alternating).

u ∧ v = −v ∧ u

This implies that:

u ∧ u = 0

1.1.3 The exterior product is distributive

(a + b)∧(c + d)=(a ∧ c)+(a ∧ d)+(b ∧ c)+(b ∧ d)

1.1.4 Expanding the exterior product of two vectors

Consider 2-dimenional vector space with the following vectors:

u = ae1 + be2

v = ce1 + de2

The exterior product is:

u ∧ v = (ae1 + be2)∧(ce1 + de2)

u ∧ v = (ae1 ∧ ce1)+(ae1 ∧ de2)+(be2 ∧ ce1)+(be2 ∧ de2)

u ∧ v = ac(e1 ∧ e1)+ad(e1 ∧ e2)+bc(e2 ∧ e1)+bd(e2 ∧ e2)

u ∧ v = ad(e1 ∧ e2)−bc(e1 ∧ e2)

u ∧ v = (ad − bc)(e1 ∧ e2)

1.1.5 Exterior (Grassman) algebra

The exterior algebra is the algebra generated by the wedge product.

The term u ∧ v can be interpreted as the area covered by the parallelogram generated by u and v.

As au ∧ bv = abu ∧ v, we can see that scaling the length of one of the vectors by a scalar, we also increase the exterior product by the same scalar.

1.1.6 Orientation

We can describe the exterior product of two vectors as u ∧ v or v ∧ u.
